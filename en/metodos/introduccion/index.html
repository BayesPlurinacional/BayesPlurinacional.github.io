Description


In everyday life and in science, we understand the world through causal arguments. We interpret observed data through causal models, which allow us to make inferences about hidden hypotheses. This is why we frequently find ourselves evaluating alternative causal models based on data.

In this programming course we aim to develop the skills to turn the computer into a tool for reasoning and deciding under uncertainty. The rules of probability, proposed in the 18th century, preserve the principle of not lying. Despite all advances in artificial intelligence, no better alternative reasoning system has been proposed in practical terms.

This course offers the first steps in this direction, as preparation for the Causal Bayesian Inference course.

Syllabus


  1. Complex systems and deterministic chaos. Simple systems can exhibit extremely complex behavior. We will build a system with a single variable modeling population size and discover the attractors of the deterministic system: fixed point, periodic and chaotic. We will see the famous butterfly effect and create pseudo-random numbers.
  2. The Galton Board and the Binomial distribution. We will simulate the ball accumulation process in the Galton Board using a random number generator. We will see what happens as we increase the number of levels and derive the exact Binomial function.
  3. The garden of forking paths. We will evaluate five causal hypotheses about the color composition of wolves in a nature reserve, updating the prior belief distribution based on the park ranger's observations.
  4. Generative causal models. We will define two alternative causal models for the Monty Hall problem and create the conditional distribution functions to simulate data from each model.
  5. Evaluating alternative causal models. We will compute the posterior belief distribution over the gift position and evaluate which of the two causal models is more consistent with the simulated data.
  6. Approximate distributions: the farmer's work. We will implement one of the oldest approximate inference methods. A farmer distributes his work across 10 orchards proportional to their size, using a random walk.
  7. Causal predictions. We will review the flow of association in the three elementary structures (fork, pipe, collider) and learn to distinguish observational predictions from causal predictions under intervention.
  8. Optimal betting. The Bayes Plurinacional community betting game: what happens over time when playing alone, as a team, and when trying to cheat.
