Plurinational Bayes

The strict application of the rules of probability (or Bayesian approach) forms the basis of artificial intelligence (AI) and empirical sciences (or data-driven sciences) because it guarantees optimal beliefs given the available information. The rules of probability have been known since the late 18th century and are conceptually intuitive: preserve the prior belief that remains compatible with the data (product rule) and predict with the contribution of all hypotheses (sum rule). While nothing better in practical terms has been proposed in all this time, their application has historically been limited due to the computational cost associated with evaluating the entire hypothesis space. On the eve of the 21st century these hurdles have been overcome in part due to modern computational and algorithmic advances. Although the growth of the Bayesian approach is currently an accelerating worldwide phenomenon, historical inertia keeps its development incipient, especially in peripheral countries.
The relevance of these methods contrasts with the current situation in which the Bayesian approach is still marginal even in the most important universities in Plurinational America, and practically non-existent in empirical sciences that do not have specific training in mathematics and programming. At the end of the century, with the process of massification of personal computers, the Bayesian approach began to take center stage in artificial intelligence until 2012, when a deep convolutional neural network trained on GPUs was presented. Again computational complexity acted as a limit to the strict application of the rules of probability, which force to evaluate the whole hypothesis space. Even so, the rise of deep neural networks did not produce the emergence of a new system of reasoning under uncertainty with better performance than that of probability rules. The Bayesian approach remains at the heart of artificial intelligence and of all empirical science. In this context, NeurlIPS held a competition in 2021 called "Approximate inference in Bayesian Deep Learning", where it is confirmed that deep ensembles are good Bayesian approximations.
Computational issues, however, are only the first step in artificial intelligence. To explain the world and make decisions in contexts of uncertainty, it is necessary to propose causal theories that predict the effect of interventions on reality. Causal theories are hypotheses, and as such can be evaluated by strict application of the rules of probability, P(Causal Theory|Data), which naturally penalizes unnecessary complexity of models. Today artificial intelligence has the opportunity to make use of causal models developed in various empirical sciences, and empirical sciences have the opportunity to create and evaluate models tailored to each specific problem. In this context, our objective is to promote Bayesian intelligence in Plurinational America and the peoples of the global south.
The word Plurinational represents in South America the coexistence of our local cultural diversities. This vision is particularly appropriate for a community working with Bayesian methods because, unlike ad-hoc procedures that select a single hypothesis (e.g. by maximum likelihood), the strict application of probability rules (Bayesian approach) forces us to consider each and every hypothesis. Just as selecting a single hypothesis has known negative consequences in probability (overfitting), the massive loss of cultural diversity caused by the imposition of a single type of society during colonial-modernity is having increasingly obvious ecological consequences. Just as Bayesian methods adapt to uncertainty while believing in mutually contradictory hypotheses, a plurinational society adapts to life through the coexistence of our diversities.
